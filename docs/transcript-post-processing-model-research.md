# On-Device Transcript Post-Processing Model — Dataset Research and Training Plan

Date: 2026-07-13  
Status: research and proposed plan; no model or training pipeline has been selected or implemented.  
Scope: open-data feasibility for a purpose-built, approximately 200–500M-parameter model that cleans Pindrop transcription output locally.

---

## 1. Executive summary

[INFERENCE] Open data appears sufficient to construct a credible English feasibility experiment and a path to multilingual coverage, but actual model feasibility remains unproven until an English pilot passes frozen behavioral and device gates. There is no single corpus that matches Pindrop's complete transformation contract.

The recommended approach is a multi-task training mixture built from:

1. real ASR hypothesis → reference pairs for recognition-error correction;
2. punctuation, capitalization, and sentence-boundary pairs;
3. spoken-form → written-form pairs for numbers, dates, times, currencies, units, and spoken punctuation;
4. disfluent → fluent pairs for fillers, repetitions, corrections, and false starts; and
5. identity examples where the input is already correct, so the model learns not to rewrite unnecessarily.

The most important data should be generated by running **Pindrop's actual shipping recognizers** over licensed audio and pairing their hypotheses with licensed reference transcripts. Public correction datasets mostly contain errors produced by Whisper, Google Cloud Speech-to-Text, or research recognizers; their error distributions will not exactly match Nemotron/Parakeet or Apple SpeechTranscriber.

[INFERENCE] The available task data is sufficient to fine-tune or distill an existing 200–500M encoder-decoder model. It is not a good basis for training a general language model of that size from random initialization; the task corpora can teach specialization, but broad language competence should come from a pretrained base.

### Recommended initial corpus

For an English feasibility study, the candidate corpus is:

- Pindrop-decoded AMI and LibriSpeech audio;
- RED-ACE;
- LibriSpeech-PC;
- LibriTTS for inverse text normalization;
- Disfl-QA for corrections and restarts;
- deterministic spoken-punctuation and number-normalization examples; and
- a substantial identity/no-op split.

Conditionally admit Common Voice only after Mozilla Data Collective terms are approved for the intended training and weight-distribution use. Admit CoVoGER only after its upstream CoVoST-2 rights chain is cleared. Admit only the specific Libriheavy manifests/subsets whose artifact and upstream provenance have been reviewed.

[INFERENCE] This candidate corpus appears sufficient to test the core question: can a small model improve recognizer output without paraphrasing, answering the speaker, or inventing content?

## 2. Product contract

Pindrop's current `Clean Transcript` preset is defined in `Pindrop/Resources/BuiltInPresets.swift:217-236`. Its current contract requires the target model to:

- fix spelling and local grammatical errors;
- insert punctuation at natural boundaries;
- restore sentence and proper-noun capitalization;
- convert number words to digits, such as `twenty-five` → `25` and `ten percent` → `10%`;
- convert spoken punctuation, such as `question mark` → `?`;
- remove filler words only when they do not carry meaning;
- preserve exact meaning and source order, without paraphrasing or reordering;
- preserve questions and commands as dictated text rather than answering or executing them; and
- return only the cleaned text.

The separate live-refinement presets add two safeguards that the current `Clean Transcript` prompt does not state: convert number words only where unambiguous, and preserve incomplete final clauses or mid-word endings rather than completing them. Both are proposed safety invariants for the new model and require explicit Phase 0 ratification. Until ratified, examples that depend on either judgment remain outside train/development/test manifests; after ratification, one policy must govern the coverage matrix, generators, and evaluation targets.

This is deliberately narrower than general grammatical-error correction or general-purpose instruction following. Fluency datasets that encourage paraphrasing are therefore a poor fit even when they produce more polished prose.

Pindrop currently exposes 16 dictation languages in `Pindrop/Services/SettingsStore.swift:97-143`:

- English;
- Russian;
- Ukrainian;
- Simplified Chinese;
- Spanish;
- French;
- German;
- Turkish;
- Japanese;
- Brazilian Portuguese;
- Italian;
- Dutch;
- Korean;
- Hindi;
- Malayalam; and
- Polish.

[INFERENCE] A path to all 16 languages appears plausible, but it depends on rights clearance, locale-specific data construction, and successful per-language pilots. Several languages will initially rely more heavily on product-generated ASR pairs and controlled synthetic data.

## 3. Research conclusions

### 3.1 There is no single complete dataset

The relevant public data is fragmented by task:

- ASR correction corpora generally target word substitutions, insertions, and deletions.
- Punctuation corpora generally remove punctuation and case from otherwise clean text.
- text-normalization corpora focus on semiotic classes such as dates, money, and measurements.
- disfluency corpora focus on corrections and restarts, often without producing a cleaned-text target.
- grammatical-error-correction corpora often rewrite style and sentence structure, which violates Pindrop's preservation contract.

The model should therefore be trained as a conservative multi-task transducer rather than as a generic rewriting assistant.

### 3.2 Product-matched hypotheses matter more than corpus size alone

A correction model learns the recognizer's characteristic errors. For the same utterance, recognizers may produce:

- `can you send it by friday`;
- `can you send it by fry day`;
- `can you send it by friday question mark`; or
- `can you send it by Friday?`.

Training only on Whisper or Google Cloud hypotheses risks optimizing for the wrong substitution, segmentation, casing, and punctuation behavior. The core dataset should be generated with every recognizer configuration Pindrop intends to support.

### 3.3 Identity examples are a first-class requirement

A sequence-to-sequence model trained only on erroneous inputs learns that every input should change. That is incompatible with the product contract. Clean input → identical output examples must be deliberately sampled and measured.

### 3.4 Open availability is not the same as commercial usability

A Hugging Face license badge or repository `LICENSE` file may apply only to packaging or code, not to upstream speech and transcripts. Aggregated corpora frequently contain LDC, TED, YouTube, or other restricted material.

The training pipeline must retain per-example provenance and permit later removal of a source. Legal status in this document is compliance triage, not legal advice.

## 6. Datasets to exclude from a commercial training pipeline

| Dataset family | Reason |
|---|---|
| GigaSpeech | Dataset terms restrict use to non-commercial research and education; the project does not own all underlying audio copyrights. The repository's Apache license applies to code, not blanket data rights. |
| Switchboard, Fisher, and NXT disfluency | LDC-gated. Public/non-member terms are non-commercial; commercial development requires a for-profit agreement and clarification for distributed model weights. |
| TED-LIUM, MuST-C, IWSLT/TED punctuation derivatives | TED content is NC-ND, and current TED terms explicitly prohibit AI/ML training without a written license. |
| Google Text Normalization and NVIDIA Numb3rs | CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. |
| BEA W&I+LOCNESS, FCE, NUCLE, JFLEG, and similar learner GEC corpora | Non-commercial/custom terms and/or fluency-oriented targets that encourage paraphrasing. |
| SponSpeech and BehancePR | Useful spontaneous punctuation data, but CC BY-NC or CC BY-NC-SA. |
| Any unfiltered general fluency corpus | Behavioral mismatch: a polished paraphrase can be worse than a minimally corrected transcript. |

Internal research use of a non-commercial corpus may also be part of commercial product development. Do not assume `evaluation only` is permitted without checking the applicable terms.

## 7. Proposed data program

### Phase 0 — freeze the product, rights, lineage, and benchmark contracts

Before downloading or transforming data:

1. Freeze the allowed and forbidden transformations. The default is the current `Clean Transcript` contract: exact meaning and source order, no paraphrasing or reordering. Decide whether to adopt incomplete-clause preservation, how to handle self-corrections such as `scratch that`, which spoken commands are supported, and whether formatting operations beyond plain-text cleanup are in scope.
2. Create a contract-coverage matrix with one row per transformation and columns for policy, direct source, synthetic generator, ambiguity rules, negative examples, evaluation slice, and owner.
3. Define a canonical example schema and a separate many-to-many rights/provenance ledger.
4. Assign a global split to each upstream utterance/document lineage before generating recognizer hypotheses, corruptions, or identity rows. Every derivative inherits that split.
5. Freeze the benchmark baselines, scoring definitions, sample sizes, statistical procedure, hardware target, and provisional pass/fail thresholds.
6. Add a hard exclude state so a source can be removed and all downstream artifacts rebuilt.
7. Separate raw licensed material, generated hypotheses, normalized training rows, and distributable evaluation/model artifacts.

Recommended minimum example schema:

```text
example_id
upstream_corpus
upstream_release
upstream_utterance_or_document_id
normalized_text_hash
audio_fingerprint
parent_example_ids
derivation_chain
split
split_policy_version
language
locale
recognizer
recognizer_version
input_text
target_text
primary_transformation_type
additional_transformation_types
speaker_or_group_id
recording_or_document_id
synthetic_or_observed
commercial_clearance_state
```

The rights ledger must support multiple records per source or example:

```text
rights_record_id
artifact_or_source_id
parent_source_id
license_or_terms_name
license_or_terms_url
terms_version_or_checked_date
scope
obligations
training_permission
raw_redistribution_permission
generated_data_permission
model_weight_distribution_permission
clearance_state
clearance_evidence
reviewer
decision_date
exclusion_reason
```

`scope` distinguishes code, manifest, audio, transcript, annotation, generated row, and model weights. `commercial_clearance_state` is one of `pending`, `cleared`, or `excluded`; only `cleared` examples may enter train, development, or test manifests.

Phase 0 acceptance criteria:

- the current or intentionally revised product contract is approved and represented in the coverage matrix;
- every example traces through its complete upstream artifact and rights chain, not just its hosting repository;
- each relevant right—training, generated-data use, raw redistribution, and model-weight distribution—has an evidenced decision;
- global lineage splits are deterministic and inherited by every derivative;
- source removal rebuilds all downstream artifacts without orphaned derivatives;
- benchmark baselines, score formulas, human-adjudication rules, hardware, and provisional thresholds are frozen before the first training comparison; and
- no raw restricted corpus is copied into a distributable artifact.

### Phase 1 — build an English feasibility corpus

Admit only rows whose `commercial_clearance_state` is `cleared`.

Immediately usable candidates, subject to recording their full rights chains:

- RED-ACE;
- Pindrop-decoded AMI and LibriSpeech;
- LibriSpeech-PC;
- LibriTTS original/normalized pairs;
- Disfl-QA;
- controlled spoken-punctuation and ITN synthesis over cleared base text; and
- identity examples from cleared clean references.

Conditional candidates remain staged outside train/development/test manifests until resolved:

- CoVoGER English ASR rows pending CoVoST-2 upstream review;
- Common Voice English pending approval of current Mozilla Data Collective terms for training and model-weight distribution; and
- a bounded Libriheavy subset pending artifact-scope and upstream-provenance review.

The first corpus should stay small enough to regenerate frequently. It should optimize for task diversity and provenance, not maximum token count.

Before generation, complete the contract-coverage matrix. It must cover spelling/local grammar, punctuation, proper-noun capitalization, ambiguous number forms, semantic versus filler uses, self-corrections, incomplete clauses, split/merged words, spoken punctuation, literal punctuation words, questions/commands, and identity behavior. If conservative grammar supervision cannot be sourced or safely synthesized, scope grammar out of the pilot instead of claiming coverage.

Phase 1 acceptance criteria:

- every in-scope transformation has a settled policy plus direct or reviewed synthetic supervision;
- every source admitted to manifests is `cleared`, not merely documented;
- recognizer-generated examples cover every shipping English recognizer selected in Phase 0;
- the seed corpus uses exactly 12% identity/no-op examples, with later experiments allowed to tune that value;
- held-out evaluation inherits global upstream-lineage splits; and
- the corpus manifest publishes source counts, transformation counts, rights states, and exclusion reasons.

### Phase 2 — generate product-matched ASR errors

For each cleared audio corpus and each shipping recognizer configuration:

1. run deterministic offline decoding;
2. retain the raw hypothesis and recognizer metadata;
3. normalize only representation details required for alignment, without silently correcting content;
4. align the hypothesis with the reference;
5. classify error spans as substitution, insertion, deletion, split, merge, punctuation, case, or normalization errors; and
6. reject reference rows whose transcript quality is insufficient.

Generate multiple acoustic/error conditions only when reproducible and licensed. Avoid producing a corpus dominated by unrealistically noisy inputs.

Until an opt-in first-party corpus exists, evaluate product match against a frozen, purpose-recorded dictation benchmark from consenting project testers rather than claiming similarity to private user sessions. Compare measurable features: utterance length, error-category frequencies, WER/CER distribution, split/merge rates, punctuation behavior, number-normalization behavior, and named-entity errors.

Phase 2 acceptance criteria:

- the Pindrop-decoded corpus is closer to the frozen dictation benchmark than public Whisper-only pairs on the predeclared distribution-distance measures;
- error categories and rates are reported per recognizer and language;
- no upstream utterance, audio fingerprint, normalized text hash, or derivative crosses global splits; and
- decoder versions and settings are pinned so the corpus can be reproduced.

### Phase 3 — train and evaluate an English pilot

[INFERENCE] Start with a pretrained 200–500M encoder-decoder architecture rather than a decoder-only chat model. The task is constrained text transduction, not open-ended generation.

Use this normalized seed mixture, sampled by example:

| Portion | Data |
|---:|---|
| 40% | Product-matched ASR hypothesis → reference pairs |
| 20% | Punctuation, capitalization, and sentence boundaries |
| 12% | Dates, times, numbers, currencies, percentages, and measurements |
| 8% | Spoken punctuation and dictation commands |
| 8% | Disfluency, corrections, repetitions, and fillers |
| 12% | Identity/no-op examples |

Each multi-label row is assigned one primary transformation for sampling and retains all additional labels for evaluation. Every experiment must persist final normalized weights, row/token counts, sampler seed, and corpus-manifest version. Later mixture searches may vary these weights, but each candidate mixture must renormalize to 100%.

Compare three frozen baselines on identical evaluation rows:

1. raw recognizer output with no post-processing;
2. deterministic rules-only punctuation/ITN processing; and
3. the current cloud AI enhancement path using the canonical `Clean Transcript` prompt and a pinned model/version where the provider permits reproducible evaluation.

The pilot passes only when it meets every provisional gate in Section 8.4. WER improvement alone is insufficient.

### Phase 4 — extend to Pindrop's language set

Recommended source order:

1. Common Voice for product-matched breadth;
2. FLEURS for balanced coverage and held-out evaluation;
3. MLS for European-language scale;
4. CoVoGER for German, Japanese, Turkish, Chinese, and English direct correction pairs;
5. UA-GEC for conservative Ukrainian spelling and punctuation;
6. locale-specific punctuation and ITN synthesis; and
7. opt-in first-party Pindrop corrections where user consent and privacy design permit.

Language-specific risks:

- Portuguese training data may skew European rather than Brazilian.
- Japanese and Chinese punctuation/segmentation cannot be modeled by translating English corruption rules.
- Russian, Hindi, Malayalam, and Korean lack strong, clearly licensed direct ITN/disfluency corpora.
- named-entity casing is language-dependent or absent in several scripts.
- number, date, time, address, and currency conventions must be locale-specific.

Each language must pass its own quality gate. Aggregate multilingual accuracy must not hide regressions in smaller languages.

### Phase 5 — build an opt-in first-party improvement loop

Open corpora will not reproduce Pindrop's exact microphones, users, vocabulary, applications, or recognizer configurations. The highest-value long-term data would be user-corrected transcript pairs collected only through an explicit privacy-preserving opt-in.

Any future collection design must define:

- whether audio is ever collected or only raw/corrected text;
- consent at collection time;
- local redaction of names, secrets, credentials, and private content;
- retention and deletion controls;
- whether data may be used for model training;
- whether users can inspect/export/delete contributed samples; and
- whether collection remains disabled by default.

This phase requires a separate privacy and product design; it is not implied by the current history store.

## 8. Evaluation plan

### 8.1 Split discipline

Assign the split at the upstream lineage before creating recognizer hypotheses, punctuation variants, synthetic corruptions, or identity rows. Every derivative inherits that split.

Within each source, group by:

- upstream utterance/document ID;
- speaker;
- recording/session;
- source document or book;
- prompt sentence where available;
- normalized text hash; and
- audio fingerprint where available.

Separately designate one or more entire cleared corpora as named out-of-domain holdouts. Do not use the ambiguous policy “split by source dataset”: within-source grouped splits and whole-corpus holdouts answer different questions and must both be explicit.

### 8.2 Required metrics

1. **Word and character error reduction** against the clean reference.
2. **Punctuation and capitalization F1.**
3. **Inverse text normalization exact match by class:** date, time, money, percentage, cardinal, ordinal, address, and measurement.
4. **Filler-deletion precision and recall.** False deletion is more damaging than leaving a filler.
5. **Identity preservation rate** on already-clean text.
6. **Named-entity preservation.**
7. **Negation, quantity, tense, and modality preservation.**
8. **Insertion/hallucination rate.**
9. **Question and command non-execution rate.**
10. **Incomplete-clause preservation.**
11. **Per-language and per-recognizer results.**
12. **Latency, peak memory, model size, first-use preparation cost, and sustained throughput on supported Macs.**

### 8.3 Contract-focused adversarial cases

The evaluation set must include examples such as:

| Raw input | Required output | Forbidden behavior |
|---|---|---|
| `what time is the meeting question mark` | `What time is the meeting?` | Answering with a time. |
| `delete the last paragraph period` | `Delete the last paragraph.` | Deleting content or interpreting the command. |
| `i do not approve this` | `I do not approve this.` | Dropping `not`. |
| `the budget is fifteen no fifty thousand` | `The budget is 50,000.` | Keeping the abandoned amount or choosing `15,000`. |
| `we should ship on fri` | `We should ship on fri` | Completing `Friday` or adding words without evidence. |
| `i like this` | `I like this.` | Removing semantic `like` as a filler. |
| `the word comma appears twice` | `The word comma appears twice.` | Replacing the literal word with punctuation. |
| already clean input | Identical output. | Cosmetic rewriting or synonym substitution. |

### 8.4 Provisional pilot gate

Phase 0 must ratify or replace these provisional values before training begins. Until then they are explicit planning defaults rather than measured product claims.

Evaluation sets:

- at least 10,000 ordinary held-out utterances across at least 500 speakers where source coverage permits;
- at least 5,000 already-clean identity examples;
- at least 2,000 contract-focused adversarial examples, including at least 500 questions/commands; and
- at least 500 examples for each supported ITN class included in the launch contract.

Quality gates:

- at least 10% relative WER reduction on rows containing lexical ASR errors, with no statistically significant aggregate WER regression across all rows;
- at least 5 absolute points of punctuation/capitalization F1 improvement over raw recognizer output;
- at least 97% exact match for each supported ITN class;
- at least 99% filler-deletion precision and at least 80% recall;
- at least 99.5% exact identity preservation;
- at most 0.1% hallucinated-content rate on ordinary held-out rows;
- zero confirmed question/command execution, negation reversal, unsupported quantity change, or invented clause on the 2,000-example contract set; and
- no per-recognizer result may miss a safety gate even when the aggregate passes.

Use paired bootstrap 95% confidence intervals for WER/CER and punctuation comparisons. Semantic and adversarial cases require two blinded reviewers plus adjudication; record the rubric and inter-reviewer agreement. Cases with multiple acceptable renderings must define an allowed-output set or use that adjudication procedure.

Provisional device gate:

- benchmark on an M1 MacBook Air with 8 GB RAM as the reference floor for Apple-silicon support;
- inputs capped at the launch contract's maximum, provisionally 512 tokens;
- warm P95 enhancement latency no greater than 250 ms;
- cold model preparation no greater than 3 seconds;
- peak additional resident memory no greater than 750 MB; and
- packaged model plus tokenizer and metadata no greater than 350 MB.

If the feature must support Intel Macs, Phase 0 must name a separate Intel reference machine and thresholds. A model that produces prettier prose but changes user intent fails regardless of aggregate quality or latency.

## 9. Model and packaging implications

This document does not select a model architecture or Apple runtime. Dataset research implies several constraints:

- The model should be optimized for bounded text-to-text transformation, not instruction-following conversation.
- The tokenizer must handle all Pindrop scripts efficiently if one multilingual model is used.
- Inputs are generally short, so a large context window is unnecessary.
- Constrained/local editing behavior is more important than broad world knowledge.
- Quantization must be evaluated against punctuation, digits, named entities, and low-resource languages, not only aggregate loss.
- A 200–500M parameter count does not directly equal download size. At four bits, raw weights are roughly 100–250 MB before tokenizer, metadata, alignment, runtime packaging, and compression overhead [INFERENCE]. At eight bits, raw weights are roughly 200–500 MB.

Architecture, Core ML/Core AI conversion, quantization, and runtime integration should be evaluated only after the English dataset and behavioral benchmark exist. Otherwise model comparisons will optimize against a moving target.

## 10. Open decisions

These decisions block Phase 0 completion:

1. Is the first release English-only, or must the first model support all 16 current dictation languages?
2. Which recognizers and versions must the model correct at launch?
3. Does the new model retain the current exact-order contract? The default is yes; permitting reordering is a product-contract change, not an implementation detail.
4. Should `scratch that` and self-corrections be resolved, or merely punctuated and preserved?
5. Which spoken-punctuation commands belong in the supported product contract?
6. Does Pindrop need formatting operations such as new paragraph, bullets, and Markdown, or only plain-text cleanup?
7. Are the provisional model-size, memory, latency, quality, and semantic-safety gates in Section 8.4 acceptable?
8. Can Common Voice's current Mozilla Data Collective terms be approved for training, generated pairs, and distribution of model weights?
9. Can CoVoGER's full upstream rights chain be cleared, or should it remain excluded?
10. Should a future opt-in correction program collect only text pairs, or audio as well?

## 11. Recommended next step

Build the rights ledger, global lineage splitter, contract-coverage matrix, and frozen English benchmark before selecting a base model:

1. ratify the product contract and provisional Section 8.4 gates;
2. ingest raw metadata for the initially clear candidates—RED-ACE, LibriSpeech-PC, LibriTTS, Disfl-QA, AMI, and LibriSpeech—while recording complete rights chains and canonical upstream lineages;
3. create the purpose-recorded dictation benchmark from consenting project testers and register each recording as an upstream lineage;
4. assign every upstream lineage to its global train/development/test split, and name whole-corpus out-of-domain holdouts, before decoding or synthetic generation;
5. stage but do not admit CoVoGER, Common Voice, or Libriheavy until their named clearance questions are resolved and their upstream lineages receive global splits;
6. decode bounded AMI, LibriSpeech, and dictation-benchmark audio with each frozen shipping Pindrop recognizer; every hypothesis inherits its parent's split;
7. generate reviewed spoken-punctuation, ITN, filler, split-word, grammar, and identity examples according to the contract-coverage matrix; every derivative inherits its parent's split; and
8. publish corpus statistics, rights decisions, exclusions, benchmark definitions, and raw/rules/cloud baseline results.

Only then compare candidate 200–500M models. The first evidence-based decision is whether a small specialized model can pass the semantic-preservation and device gates, not which runtime or architecture is most convenient.
